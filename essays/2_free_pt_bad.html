we can't afford free public transport

Another Reddit comment series on public transport and urban planning. Here you go.

People don't seem to understand that the greatest barrier to public transport use isn't the cost – especially right now when fuel prices are sky high. It's the abysmal service. People will not use a service that, for example, in my area, can take three times as long to get to a destination compared to a car (20 min vs 1 hour).

Pre-COVID, PTV fare revenue was approaching a billion dollars a year, and there's way better things a government could do if they wanted to invest a billion dollars in public transport. A few years ago the Parliamentary Budget Office estimated it'd cost just $200 million to get us trains and trams every 10 minutes seven days a week (6am-9pm) and to run them at max capacity during peak hour, which alone would be a game changer; and even then you'd still have $800 million left. Imagine $800 million pumped into our state's bus networks – that's the sort of stuff that will actually get people out of their cars and onto public transport.

The assumption is that given that the government earns $1 billion from fares, should you take it away, you would need $1 billion in extra funding to make up for the shortfall. The assumptions here are deliberately simplistic and it's more to make a point of 'what could be a better use of that much extra money on something public transport-related?'

To say analysis has gone into current operating schedules is a stretch. When it's done, it happens within the confines that the Department of Transport sets, and it's about operator stuff, like making sure trains don't conflict; the tendency seems to be that many other considerations are often ignored. And for buses, a lot of them don't even get reviewed for ages; there are loads of buses in Melbourne with slow, winding, indirect routes as a carryover from when buses were unregulated, for instance.

In any case, public transport is a right that isn't meant to be profit-making, in the same vein as health care or public housing. People shouldn't have to endure public transport—because endure is what it currently is—just because they don't own a car. Being convenient for the public is exactly the point: if you provide better quality, more frequent services, people will use them.
